Background

About Me

I grew up in India, where motorsport isn't a career path — it's something you watch from the other side of a screen. I was the kid with his face buried in Autocar magazine, memorising engine specs for cars I'd never see in person. My friends called me the car encyclopaedia. It was a passion with nowhere obvious to go.

Getting into Oxford Brookes to study Racing Engine Systems changed that. I relocated from India to the UK, built the skills, and committed fully. The career I used to watch through a screen is now the one I'm training for every day.

My engineering identity sits at the intersection of precision CAD design, structural analysis, and systematic fault investigation. I've worked on components for human spaceflight applications. I've caught a material impurity by noticing a discrepancy in a vibration model. I design things that need to work first time.

My long-term goal is to contribute to power unit and mechanical design in motorsport — not as a spectator, but as one of the engineers building what goes in the car.

Intake Manifold Modelling & Volumetric Efficiency Analysis

Built a fully parameterised GT-Power model of the BMW M52 6-cylinder intake system, using a physical manifold in the university thermodynamics lab as the dimensional reference. Investigated how runner length, diameter, and plenum volume affect volumetric efficiency across engine speeds — going beyond standard analysis by applying R Studio signal processing (Intrinsic Mode Function decomposition) to extract clean pressure pulse data from noisy runner measurements.

GT-Power R Studio / IMF Intake Geometry VE Analysis FFT
Key Findings
  • Short runners favour high-RPM VE; longer runners boost low-RPM breathing — validating variable intake tuning principles.
  • IMF signal processing revealed intake pressure resonance events otherwise masked by measurement noise.
  • Linked plenum volume to HC/CO emissions behaviour at lower engine speeds.

⬡   Material Impurity Detection — DRDO Vibration Analysis

During industrial training at DRDO's Vibration & Acoustics Lab (ENTEST Division), I performed vibration analysis of an aluminium beam in free-free suspension conditions using three independent methods: Euler-Bernoulli hand calculations, ANSYS FEA, and experimental impact hammer testing. The hand calculations and experimental results agreed — but the MATLAB model showed a fundamental frequency approximately 100 Hz lower. Rather than dismiss this as a modelling error, I investigated the root cause.

The discrepancy traced back to Young's modulus. The MATLAB model used the specified material (AL 2014), but the beam's actual elastic behaviour didn't match. The supplier's aluminium contained impurity levels significant enough to measurably alter the material's stiffness. Findings were escalated formally: the supplier was notified, the material batch was reclassified, and future structural models were corrected. This is what systematic cross-validation looks like in practice.

Dynamic Analysis of Continuous Beam — Free-Free Boundary Conditions

Vibration analysis and experimental modal testing of a 4.13 m aluminium beam suspended in free-free conditions using bungee cables. Three methods were applied and cross-validated: analytical Euler-Bernoulli calculations, ANSYS APDL FEA (10-section mesh), and experimental impact hammer testing with accelerometers and Simcentre Testpress data acquisition. It was the cross-validation process that surfaced a critical material non-conformance — detailed in the Fault Investigation note above.

ANSYS APDL MATLAB Modal Testing FRF Analysis Euler-Bernoulli
Outcomes
  • Predicted and experimentally confirmed natural frequencies and mode shapes for first five modes.
  • FRF analysis via Simcentre Testpress; IMF decomposition used to isolate relevant frequency content.
  • Identified and escalated material non-conformance with formal root cause documentation.
